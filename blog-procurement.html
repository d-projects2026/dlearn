Procurement Strategy & Methods

How you buy is as important as what you buy.

Single-stage and two-stage tendering, design-build, framework agreements, and competitive tension.

⏱ 11 min read✓ FREE

Procurement Strategy and Project Fit

Procurement method must match your project needs. Do you have a detailed design? Are cost and quality both critical? Is speed important? Is certainty preferred over low cost? Different methods answer these differently. Single-stage tendering is traditional: design is complete, you tender (invite bids) to multiple contractors, select the lowest compliant bid. This ensures cost certainty but requires you to have design finalized (takes time). Two-stage tendering allows contractor input during design: stage 1 (shortlist contractors based on capability), stage 2 (negotiate detailed price and program with chosen contractors). This is faster and allows design optimization for buildability. Design-build: you contract a single entity (contractor + designer) to deliver a completed building to your performance spec. This shifts design risk to the contractor but is fastest and allows them to optimize their own way. Framework agreements: you establish relationships and rates with multiple suppliers; work orders are issued as needed. This is flexible and good for ongoing programs. Each method has cost, time, risk, and quality trade-offs.

Tendering Process and Bid Management

1

Prepare Tender Documentation

Tender documents must be clear and complete. Include: project brief, design (to the extent complete), technical specifications, commercial terms (payment terms, insurance, liability), program, and bid procedures. Ambiguous tender documents lead to high bids (contractors protect themselves) and disputes (bidders interpret requirements differently).

2

Advertise and Invite Tenders

For public projects, advertising is mandated; for private projects, you can select your bidders (shortlist). A shortlist of 3-5 is typical. Too many bidders (10+) is expensive for them and adds little value. Too few bidders reduces competitive tension. Set clear bid deadlines and submission procedures.

3

Evaluate Bids and Select Contractor

Evaluation criteria: price is important but not only criterion. Also evaluate contractor capability (experience, resources, team quality), program (realistic schedule), technical approach (buildability, innovation), and commercial terms (payment terms, insurance adequacy). Use a scoring matrix to evaluate objectively. Lowest price alone often leads to problems: the cheapest contractor is cheap because they are cutting corners or are inexperienced.

4

Negotiate and Finalize Contract

Rarely is the first bid final. Negotiate with the leading bidder(s) to improve terms. Clarify ambiguities, align understanding, and finalize details. Negotiation should be transparent (both sides know what is negotiable). Once a contract is signed, enforce it fairly but firmly.

Framework Agreements and Long-Term Relationships

For ongoing work (maintenance, services, or repeated small projects), framework agreements are efficient. You establish rates and general terms with multiple suppliers. When work is needed, you issue a work order at pre-agreed rates. This reduces tendering cycle time and builds relationships. Suppliers benefit from predictability (steady work) and invest in understanding your operations. You benefit from speed and relationship knowledge. Framework agreements require trust — you are betting that the supplier will deliver consistently. Choose suppliers carefully. Review performance regularly and adjust or terminate if performance drops. Good framework relationships often result in better prices over time as suppliers become more efficient serving you.

Maintaining Competitive Tension

💡 PRO TIP

Competition drives good pricing and performance. Even in framework agreements, periodically tender to the open market to maintain awareness of alternatives. Suppliers perform better when they know alternatives exist.

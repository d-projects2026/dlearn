Bookkeeping & Accounting Basics

The log book keeps the ship honest — every coin in, every coin out, recorded faithfully.

Debits and credits, chart of accounts, invoicing, reconciliation, and keeping your books clean.

⏱ 10 min read✓ FREE

Why Bookkeeping Matters for Every Business

Bookkeeping is the systematic recording of every financial transaction in your business. It is not glamorous, but it is the foundation of financial health. Without accurate books, you cannot prepare tax returns, apply for loans, track profitability, manage cash flow, or make informed business decisions. Many small business owners avoid bookkeeping until tax season — then scramble to reconstruct a year of transactions from bank statements and shoe boxes of receipts. This is stressful, error-prone, and expensive if you are paying an accountant to sort it out. The alternative is simple: record transactions consistently as they happen, and your books stay clean with minimal effort.
💡 PRO TIP

Set aside 30 minutes every Friday to enter the week's transactions. This one habit will save you dozens of hours at year-end.

The Basics: Debits and Credits

1

The Fundamental Equation

Assets = Liabilities + Equity. Every transaction affects at least two accounts and must keep this equation balanced. When you buy equipment with cash, assets change form but total assets stay the same: Equipment goes up, Cash goes down.

2

Debits and Credits

A debit increases assets and expenses. A credit increases liabilities, equity, and revenue. Every transaction has equal debits and credits — this double-entry system is self-checking. If your books do not balance, an error occurred somewhere.

3

The Chart of Accounts

Your chart of accounts is the organized list of all accounts in your business: Bank Account, Accounts Receivable, Inventory, Equipment, Accounts Payable, Sales Revenue, Rent Expense, Salary Expense, etc. Set it up once, customize it to your business, and use it consistently.

4

Reconciliation

Monthly bank reconciliation compares your book balance to your bank statement. Differences reveal missed transactions, duplicates, or errors. It is the single most important quality check in bookkeeping.

Invoicing and Accounts Receivable

An invoice is your formal request for payment. It should include your business details, the client's details, invoice number, date, itemized description of goods or services, amounts, applicable taxes, payment terms, and payment method. Payment terms (Net 15, Net 30, Net 60) define when payment is due. Shorter terms improve cash flow but may not be competitive. Track every invoice in an accounts receivable ledger: date issued, amount, due date, and date paid. Follow up on overdue invoices promptly — the longer you wait, the harder it becomes to collect. A polite reminder at day 7 and a firm follow-up at day 14 past due catches most issues early.
💡 PRO TIP

Number your invoices sequentially (INV-001, INV-002) and never reuse a number. It creates a clean audit trail and makes reconciliation simple.

Choosing the Right Tools

You do not need expensive software to start. A well-organized spreadsheet can handle bookkeeping for a small business. But as you grow, dedicated accounting software like QuickBooks, Xero, Wave (free), or Zoho Books saves time and reduces errors. These tools automate bank feeds, generate invoices, track expenses, and produce financial reports at the click of a button. If your business has more than a handful of transactions per week, the investment in accounting software pays for itself quickly. Choose based on your needs: number of transactions, multi-currency support, inventory tracking, payroll integration, and tax compliance requirements for your region.

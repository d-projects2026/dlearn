Copywriting: Words That Sell

The most persuasive pirates don't shout their demands—they speak words that make others want to follow them. Similarly, effective copywriting uses psychology and clarity to inspire action.

Learn the art of persuasive writing that moves people from interest to action. Master proven formulas, psychology, and techniques that separate winning copy from boring.

What Makes Copy Work

Copywriting is writing designed to persuade. It's different from journalism, which aims to inform, or novels, which aim to entertain. Sales pages, email marketing, ads, and product descriptions are all copywriting. Good copy doesn't trick people—it removes objections and clarifies why an offer is valuable. Most copy fails because it focuses on features ('This has 5GB storage') instead of benefits ('You'll never run out of space for important memories'). Customers don't care about features; they care about what features do for them. A car has 'high-torque engine' (feature) and therefore 'goes 0-60 in 4 seconds' (benefit). But the deeper benefit is 'You'll feel alive behind the wheel' or 'You'll impress people at every light.' Understanding customer motivations—what they actually want—is fundamental. Are they buying convenience? Status? Security? Health? Belonging? The same product appeals to different motivations for different customers.

The Structure of Persuasive Copy

Effective copy follows a predictable pattern: Headline grabs attention and promises a benefit. Most people scan; a compelling headline determines whether they keep reading. Subheadline (optional) clarifies the headline or adds credibility. Body copy explains the problem, positions your solution, and builds desire. Call-to-action tells readers exactly what to do next. P.S. (postscript) adds a final reason to act—often the most read part. This isn't rigid—different situations require different structures. A long-form sales page might have multiple sections building a case. An email is shorter and punchier. An ad is concise and benefit-focused. But the principle remains: attention, interest, desire, action.

Psychology and Persuasion Principles

Copywriting leverages psychological principles that influence decision-making. Scarcity creates urgency—'Limited spots available' or 'This price expires Friday.' Urgency prompts action rather than deliberation. Social proof shows others trust you—testimonials, customer counts, or celebrity endorsements. People want what others want. Authority builds credibility—credentials, experience, or expert status. People trust experts more than strangers. Reciprocity makes people want to return favors—giving free value increases willingness to buy. Liking increases persuasion—people are more influenced by people they like. Consistency activates a desire to follow through—small commitments lead to larger ones. One technique is lowballing: get someone to agree to a big commitment initially, then reveal a smaller (real) commitment, making the smaller seem reasonable by comparison. Another is anchoring: the first price mentioned anchors subsequent perceptions. Ethical copywriters use these principles honestly to clarify genuine value. Unethical copywriters manipulate. You can persuade ethically.

Writing That Converts

Conversion is the percentage of people who take your desired action (buy, signup, click). High conversion copy shares characteristics. It's benefit-focused, addressing customer desires. It's concise, respecting reader time. It uses specific language—'increases energy' is vague; 'provides 8-hour sustained energy without crashes' is specific and believable. It uses active voice ('Our system saves 10 hours weekly') instead of passive ('10 hours can be saved weekly'). It addresses objections preemptively ('You might worry about setup time, but our process takes 10 minutes'). It uses power words that evoke emotion—'exclusive,' 'breakthrough,' 'proven,' 'effortless.' It includes numbers and specificity. '90% faster' converts better than 'much faster.' It creates urgency and scarcity appropriately. It uses social proof—real testimonials, customer counts, trust badges. Testing and iteration improve conversion. Small changes—different headlines, button colors, copy length—sometimes dramatically increase conversion. The best copywriters test constantly.

💡
Pro Tip: Write benefit-focused headlines. Instead of 'Software for Project Management,' write 'Ship Projects 40% Faster and Stop Missing Deadlines.' The second speaks to what customers actually want.
💡
Pro Tip: Every sentence should move someone toward action or support a key claim. If a sentence doesn't earn its place, cut it. Concise copy respects time and converts better than bloated copy.

Steps to Write Converting Copy

1

Define Your Ideal Customer and Their Motivation

Who are you selling to? What problem do they face? What do they really want—security, status, belonging, results? Write a profile of your ideal customer and their deepest desire. This focus makes copy authentic.

2

Brainstorm Strong Headlines

Write 20 headlines. Each should promise a clear benefit. Use specific numbers, emotional words, and curiosity. Bad: 'Our Software.' Good: 'Finally, Email That Doesn't Steal Your Sanity.' Pick the strongest and refine it.

3

Create an Outline of Your Argument

What problem do you solve? Why is this problem important? How does your solution work? Why you over competitors? What's the special offer? What's the call-to-action? Outline your persuasive flow before writing.

4

Write First Draft Without Editing

Write the body copy in one sitting without stopping to edit. Get your ideas flowing. You'll polish later. Editing while writing creates blocks.

5

Edit for Conciseness and Clarity

Cut redundancy. Replace weak words with strong ones. Break long sentences into shorter ones. Read aloud to find awkward phrasing. Remove anything that doesn't support your main point.

Common Copywriting Mistakes

Feature-focused copy is the cardinal sin. Features alone don't sell; benefits do. Focusing on what's true about your product instead of what customers want is backward. Unclear value proposition makes potential customers wonder why they should care. Be explicit about the benefit. Weak call-to-action leaves people unclear about next steps. Don't say 'Learn more' vaguely; say 'Book a free consultation' or 'Start your free trial today.' Too much copy loses readers; too little leaves objections unaddressed. Test length, but generally, as long as needed and no longer. Forgetting objections means losing sales to skeptical prospects. Address concerns preemptively. Fake testimonials or claims destroy credibility if discovered. Always use real testimonials and substantiate claims.

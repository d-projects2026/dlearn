Measure twice, estimate once, build with confidence.

The Role of the Quantity Surveyor

A quantity surveyor (QS) is part contract manager, part accountant, part negotiator. Their core responsibility is accurate measurement and valuation of construction work. During design, the QS prepares estimates and cost plans. Before tender, they prepare the Bill of Quantities (BoQ) — a detailed, itemized list of every component the contractor must supply and install. During construction, the QS values work completed and processes payment claims. At the end, they prepare the final account, reconciling the contract sum against actual work done, variations, and claims. A good QS saves money through accurate measurement (preventing over-payment for work not done), smart cost planning (identifying expensive assumptions), and fair variation assessment (preventing disputes).

Bill of Quantities: Structure and Standards

1

Standard Format and Measurement Rules

Bills follow SMM (Standard Method of Measurement — varies by country). Each item includes: description, quantity, unit of measurement. Descriptions must be precise. Not just 'concrete floor' but 'concrete floor, 150mm thick, reinforced with A393 mesh, finished smooth, per specification section 4.2.' Quantities follow strict rules: floor area, not volume; linear meters for walls, not areas; each item separate even if identical.

2

Pricing and Bill Preparation

The contractor prices each line. Total = quantity × rate + overhead + profit. A well-prepared BoQ prevents disputes because it's explicit what is included in each item. If an item is omitted from the BoQ, it may still be the contractor's obligation, or it may be a variation. Ambiguity costs everyone money. The QS works with designers to translate the specification into the BoQ.

3

Provisional Sums and Prime Cost Items

Some items are uncertain at tender. Provisional sums are contingency allowances — the QS estimates based on experience. Prime cost items are for items not yet designed (e.g., electrical by a specialist). Contractors add a markup. At the end, the QS reconciles actual vs provisional, adjusting the final account.

4

Schedule of Rates

For framework agreements, the QS prepares a schedule of rates — unit prices for different elements. When work orders are issued, the rates are applied. This allows flexibility while maintaining cost control.

Interim Valuation and Payment Processing

Monthly (or milestone-based), the contractor submits an Application for Payment. The QS verifies what work is actually complete, accurate measurement, quality meets spec. The valuation includes the monthly work plus cumulative. The QS deducts retention (often 5-10% held back until practical completion as security). Interim certificates authorize payment. The QS must protect the client — paying only for work actually done. But they must also be fair to the contractor — releasing funds on time to maintain cash flow. Disputed interim valuations create tension. Clear measurement criteria (work must be 100% complete before valuation, not partially complete) prevent disputes.

Final Account and Closeout

💡 PRO TIP

Final accounts should be agreed within 6 months of practical completion. Delays create disputes. Build agreement throughout the project; do not save all adjustments for the end.

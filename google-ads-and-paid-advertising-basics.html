Google Ads & Paid Advertising Basics

A merchant might wait for customers to find them naturally, but smart merchants place ads where customers are actively searching.

Learn to use Google Ads and paid advertising effectively. Start with small budgets, measure results precisely, and scale what works.

⏱️ 11 min read
FREE

Why Paid Advertising Matters

Organic reach—getting customers without paid ads—is increasingly difficult. Social media algorithms prioritize paid content. Search results increasingly show ads. Building organic audience takes months. Paid advertising gets results quickly. If you have a budget, paid ads can drive immediate traffic and sales. The challenge is ROI. You want ads to generate more revenue than they cost. A $100 ad spend should generate $200+ in sales to be profitable (assuming $100 profit per sale). Beginners often fail because they optimize for clicks instead of sales. A cheap click that doesn't convert is worthless. Expert advertisers optimize for value—they're willing to pay more per click if it generates more sales. Google Ads, Facebook/Instagram ads, LinkedIn ads, and TikTok ads are the main platforms. Each has strengths. Google Ads reaches people actively searching for solutions. Facebook/Instagram reaches people based on interests and demographics. LinkedIn reaches business professionals. TikTok reaches younger audiences. Choose based on where your customers are.

Understanding Google Ads

Google Ads has two main types: Search ads and Display ads. Search ads appear when people Google relevant keywords. If you sell shoes and someone searches 'running shoes near me,' your ad might appear at the top. Search ads are highest-intent because people are actively searching for solutions. You bid on keywords; higher bids get better placement. You only pay when someone clicks your ad. Display ads appear on websites across Google's network. They're less direct but reach more people. You bid based on impressions or clicks. Remarketing ads show to people who previously visited your website—useful for people who didn't convert initially. The cost of ads varies by keyword and competition. Competitive keywords cost more. 'Insurance' might cost $5-50 per click; obscure keywords might cost $.10. Start with specific keywords related to your product. Broad keywords are expensive; specific ones are cheaper but reach fewer people. A good starting strategy: use specific keywords with lower cost-per-click while you optimize. Google shows your ads based on ad quality score (relevance, click-through rate) and your bid amount. Better ads with high relevance scores get better placement and lower costs. Investing in compelling ad copy and relevant landing pages improves scores and reduces costs.

Creating Effective Ads

Ad copy determines whether people click. Headline should match what they searched. If someone searches 'affordable project management software,' your headline should echo this: 'Affordable Project Management - $99/month.' This relevance increases click-through rates and quality scores. Ad copy should highlight your unique value. Why choose you over competitors? Include a call-to-action: 'Start Free Trial,' 'Shop Now,' or 'Schedule Demo.' Ad extensions add extra information: site links, phone numbers, location, or pricing. These increase click rates and improve quality scores. Landing page quality matters as much as ad quality. When someone clicks your ad, they should land on a page directly related to the ad. If your ad says 'Free Project Management Trial' but your landing page is your homepage, that mismatch increases bounce rate and lowers conversions. Create specific landing pages for ad campaigns. The landing page should be simple, focused, with a clear call-to-action. Remove navigation and distractions. The only goal: get visitors to sign up or purchase. This focus increases conversion.

Budgets, Bids, and Optimization

Set a daily budget you're comfortable losing. Google will spend up to that daily limit across your ads. Start small—$10-50 daily—to test before scaling. You can increase budget when you find profitable keywords. Bid strategy determines how you pay. Cost-per-click (CPC) means you pay each time someone clicks. Cost-per-action (CPA) means you set a target cost for conversions and Google optimizes toward that. CPA requires conversion tracking setup but is powerful—Google automatically bids to hit your target cost per sale. Conversion tracking is critical. Set up tracking so you know which ads, keywords, and landing pages produce sales. Without tracking, you're guessing. Google Analytics integrated with Google Ads reveals what happens after clicks—do people buy or bounce? Optimization is ongoing. Which keywords generate sales cheaply? Increase budget there. Which generate clicks but no sales? Pause them. Keywords that convert get budget increases; wasteful ones get paused. Over months, your ad portfolio becomes increasingly efficient. Start with broad testing: try multiple keywords, different ad copy, different landing pages. Measure results. Keep what works; pause what doesn't. This iterative approach finds profitable combinations.

💡
Pro Tip: Quality score matters as much as bid amount. An ad with high quality score (10/10) gets better placement at lower cost than a low-quality score ad (3/10) with higher bids. Focus on relevance and landing page quality.
💡
Pro Tip: Negative keywords prevent wasted spending. If you sell premium coaching and people search 'free coaching,' you don't want your ads there. Add 'free' as a negative keyword so your ads don't appear for those searches.

Steps to Launch Your First Google Ads Campaign

1

Create Google Ads Account and Set Up Conversion Tracking

Sign up for Google Ads. Link to your website. Set up conversion tracking (tracking sales, signups, or whatever your goal is). Without tracking, you can't measure success.

2

Create a Campaign and Identify Keywords

Create a Search Ads campaign. Brainstorm keywords people search when looking for your solution. Include different variations (specific, broad, phrase match). Start with 10-20 keywords.

3

Write Ad Copy and Create Landing Page

Write ad headlines and descriptions that highlight your value. Create a focused landing page directly aligned with the ad. Keep it simple with one clear call-to-action.

4

Set Budget and Bids

Set a daily budget you're comfortable with ($10-50 to start). Set bids for keywords or use Google's automated bidding. Expect learning—costs per conversion will decrease over time as Google optimizes.

5

Monitor and Optimize

Check performance daily for the first week. Which keywords get clicks? Which convert? Pause low-performing keywords. Increase bids on keywords that convert cheaply. Adjust landing page based on bounce rate data.

Common Paid Advertising Mistakes

Broad keywords that attract irrelevant traffic waste budget. Be specific. Weak landing pages lose conversions even if ads are great. Invest in landing pages. Not tracking conversions means flying blind. You won't know what works. Increasing budget too quickly can waste money before you optimize. Start small, learn what works, then scale. Expecting immediate results is unrealistic. Good ads take time to optimize. Set realistic expectations and give campaigns 1-2 weeks before judging. Ads with unclear value proposition don't convert. Be specific about why someone should choose you. Not testing variations means missing improvements. Test different ad copy, headlines, landing pages, and keywords.

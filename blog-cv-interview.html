CV & Interview Preparation

Your CV opens the door — the interview is where you walk through it.

Writing standout resumes, the STAR method, common questions, and making a lasting impression.

⏱ 10 min read✓ FREE

Crafting a CV That Gets Noticed

Recruiters spend an average of six to eight seconds on the first scan of a CV. In those seconds, they decide whether to read further or move on. This means your CV must be visually clean, well-organized, and front-loaded with your most impressive qualifications. Lead with a professional summary — two to three sentences that capture who you are, what you do, and what makes you valuable. Follow with key skills, then work experience in reverse chronological order, then education and certifications. Keep it to two pages maximum for experienced professionals, one page for early-career. Every line should earn its place.
💡 PRO TIP

Customize your CV for each application. Mirror the language in the job description — if they say 'stakeholder management,' use that exact phrase, not 'relationship building.'

Writing Achievement-Based Bullets

1

Start with a Strong Verb

Managed, delivered, reduced, increased, designed, implemented, negotiated, led, established. Avoid passive language like 'responsible for' or 'involved in.' You did things — say so directly.

2

Quantify Everything

Numbers create credibility: 'Managed a $25M infrastructure project,' 'Reduced procurement cycle time by 30%,' 'Led a team of 15 engineers.' Even approximate numbers are better than vague descriptions.

3

Show Impact

Do not just list duties — show results. 'Implemented a new QA/QC process that reduced defect rates by 40% and saved $200K in rework costs' tells a story of value. 'Managed quality' does not.

4

Tailor to the Role

If the job requires cost management experience, lead with your cost management achievements. If it requires leadership, highlight your team leadership stories. Put the most relevant content first.

The STAR Interview Method

Behavioral interview questions — 'Tell me about a time when...' — are best answered using the STAR framework. Situation: briefly set the scene. Task: what was your specific responsibility? Action: what did you do? Result: what was the outcome? Prepare five to seven STAR stories from your experience covering common themes: leadership, conflict resolution, tight deadlines, difficult stakeholders, innovation, and failure/learning. Practice telling each story in two minutes or less. The structure keeps you focused and ensures you always end with a concrete result rather than trailing off.
💡 PRO TIP

Record yourself practicing STAR answers on your phone. It feels awkward but reveals filler words, rambling, and weak endings that you can fix before the real interview.

Interview Day Essentials

Preparation goes beyond knowing your answers. Research the company thoroughly — recent projects, company values, leadership team, current challenges. Prepare three to five thoughtful questions to ask the interviewer — it shows genuine interest. Arrive ten minutes early (or test your video setup 15 minutes before for virtual interviews). Dress one level above the company's standard dress code. Bring extra copies of your CV, a notebook, and a pen. During the interview, maintain eye contact, listen carefully to each question, and take a moment to think before answering — rushing signals nervousness. After the interview, send a brief thank-you email within 24 hours, referencing a specific conversation point.
